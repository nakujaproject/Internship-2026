### Abstract
This experiment aims to investigate the microscopic structure of KNSB (Potassium Nitrate – Sorbitol) propellant grains to identify defects such as voids, air bubbles, and microcracks that contribute to rapid pressure spikes and CATO failures. A standard reference grain will be produced using optimized casting and curing methods. The grain will be sectioned and examined under a microscope. A simple strand burn rate test will also be performed to correlate microstructure with combustion behavior. Results will inform improved grain fabrication protocols for the 5-grain motor.

---

### 1. Introduction
KNSB is our chosen composite propellant (65% KNO₃ oxidizer + 35% sorbitol fuel). Past static tests have shown CATO failures linked to suspected internal defects causing sudden increases in burning surface area and chamber pressure. Understanding grain microstructure is critical for mitigating these issues before full motor production.

---

### 2. Objectives
1. Produce a high-quality reference KNSB grain using standardized procedures.
2. Examine the internal microstructure (voids, cracks, crystal distribution) under a microscope.
3. Perform a simple burn rate test to correlate microstructure with combustion performance.
4. Establish baseline data for comparison with improved curing methods (pressure, vacuum, slow cooling, additives).

---

### 3. Hypothesis
- **Primary Hypothesis**: Grains produced with pressure curing and vacuum degassing will exhibit significantly fewer voids and microcracks compared to standard room-temperature cured grains.
- **Secondary Hypothesis**: Reduced defects will result in more consistent burn rates and lower risk of pressure spikes during combustion.

---

### 4. Materials
- Potassium Nitrate (finely milled KNO₃)
- Sorbitol
- Liner
- Microscope 
- Cutting tools (saw or blade for sectioning)
- Burn rate test setup (simple strand holder, stopwatch, ruler)
- Safety equipment (PPE, fume hood)

---

### 5. Methods & Procedure

#### Phase 1: Reference Grain Preparation (Standard Method)
1. Melt sorbitol at ~120°C.
2. Gradually add finely milled KNO₃ while stirring to achieve uniform 65/35 mixture.
3. Pour into liner cast.
4. Cure under pressure (threaded rod + plate) for 1 week.
5. Record: mass, dimensions, density.

#### Phase 2: Microscopic Analysis
1. Carefully section the cured grain into thin slices (longitudinal and cross-sectional).
2. Prepare samples on glass slides.
3. Observe under microscope at 40x–400x magnification.
4. Document:
   - Void size and distribution
   - Microcracks
   - Crystal uniformity
   - Density variations
5. Take high-resolution photos with scale.

#### Phase 3: Simple Burn Rate Test
1. Cut a small strand (~5–10 mm long, uniform cross-section) from the reference grain.
2. Mount vertically in a holder.
3. Ignite one end and record burn time with stopwatch.
4. Measure burned length.
5. Calculate linear burn rate (\( r = \frac{\text{length burned}}{\text{time}} \)).
6. Repeat 3–5 times for average.

---

### 6. Data Collection & Analysis
- **Microscopic Data**: Number of voids per field, average void size, crack density.
- **Burn Rate Data**: Average \( r \) (mm/s), consistency across samples.
- **Comparison**: Compare with previous grains and simulations.
- **Tools**: Google Sheets for data logging, microscope software for measurements.

---

### 7. Safety Protocols
- Conduct all propellant handling in fume hood with PPE.
- Small sample sizes only for burn rate test.
- Fire extinguisher and sand bucket nearby.
- No testing near main motor components.
